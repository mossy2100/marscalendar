Before we I can explain how the calendar works, it is useful to review the Martian astronomical cycles on which it is based, and the units used in the explanation of the calendar.

  • A Martian day is called a “sol”, and has an average duration of 88 775.244 09 seconds, which is 1.027 49 days, or 24 hours, 39 minutes, and 35.244 09 seconds. Thus, a sol is only about 40 minutes longer than an Earth day. The word “sol” was introduced by NASA, and has been used to refer to a Martian solar day since the Viking missions. The fact that a sol is almost the same length as a Terran day is tremendously convenient when designing a calendar, because it permits the creation of one very similar to those we’re already familiar with, which have as their fundamental unit the Terran solar day, the dominant cycle of human behaviour and society.
  • Each sol is divided into decisols, millisols, and tals. This is discussed further in the section about Time of the sol.
  • As part of this calendar I introduced the term “mir” for “Martian year”. “Mir” is a Russian word that means “world”, “peace”, and “community”, and was the name of the gone-but-never-forgotten legendary Russian space station. It means “community” in the sense of a community combining their efforts for a common task, such as building a church or clearing a field. The word “mir” not only sounds like a perfect contraction of “Martian year”, but also invokes the spirit of a group working together with the common cause of developing Mars into a peaceful world community. A mir has about 668.5907 sols on average, which is equal to about 687 days or 1.88 years. With SI prefixes the terms decamir, hectomir, and kilomir are derived, for Martian decade, century, and millennium.
  • Martian “weeks” and “months” do not refer to precise periods of time, but rather to periods similar in duration and purpose to weeks and months on Earth.
