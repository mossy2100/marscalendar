Notation for Utopian dates is similar to the international standard ISO 8601 for Gregorian date, time, and datetime strings, with a few changes:

  • The date, time, or datetime string begins with ‘M’, to indicate that it relates to Mars.
  • Mir numbers do not need to be zero-padded to a fixed number of digits. The current mir number (216 at the time of writing) has 3 digits, but this is written as M216, not M0216. When it reaches 1000, it will be M1000. This is for future-proofing. Eventually mir numbers will have 4 digits, 5 digits, and so on. It’s actually a flaw in ISO 8601 that years must be expressed as exactly four digits, because the standard will not work from the year 10 000. The creators created a Y10K problem. They should remove this restriction from the standard, and prefix the year number with ‘E’ to distinguish an Terran date from a Martian date.
  • Month and sol parts of the date also don’t need to be zero-padded to 2 digits. e.g. M216/1/5
  • Negative mir numbers are permitted, e.g. M-100/10/5
  • Times are displayed as a decimal fraction of a sol, usually to 3 or 5 decimal places, to indicate mils or tals respectively, e.g. M.543
  • The date separator character is ‘/’. On Earth we use hyphen (or minus sign) ‘-’, forward slash ‘/’, and period (or decimal point) ‘.’ characters as date separators, depending on the country. The ISO 8601 standard uses the hyphen (e.g. “2016-12-06”). However, the hyphen and period characters have other meanings within the format, as a negative sign and a decimal point respectively, therefore we should choose an alternative for clarity. The forward slash is the most obvious choice, as it clearly indicates a date when used in this way. A different character could potentially be used, e.g. 216^17^22 or 216*17*22, but this might be tedious to write, less likely to be adopted, and unclear as to its meaning (e.g. potentially interpreted as a mathematical operator). The ‘M’ prefix will prevent confusion with Terran dates better than an unusual separator.
  • A date and time can be combined into a single datetime string, e.g. M216/17/22.12345
  • Thousands and thousandths separators are not used and are invalid.

Thus, the complete format description looks like:

M{date}{time}

Where:

{date} = {mir}/{month}/{sol}

{mir} = an integer (negative, 0, or positive), with no thousands separators

{month} = an integer in the range 1 to 24

{sol} = an integer in the range 1 to 28

{time} = .{digits} (i.e. a decimal point followed by at least one digit, and no thousandths separators)

Both {date} and {time} are optional, but obviously not both.

Examples:

Date: M216/17/8

Date in negative mir: M-32/17/8

Time, resolution mils: M.543

Time, resolution tals: M.54321

Datetime, resolution mils: M216/17/8.123

Datetime, resolution tals: M216/17/8.12345

Presumably the ISO 8601 standard requires date and time parts to have fixed widths in order to simplify parsing, but the above format is easily parsed and more compact.

Times will normally be expressed to 3 decimal places to indicate mils. Comparing Earth times of day with Mars:

00:00 = M.000

06:00 = M.250

12:00 = M.500

18:00 = M.750
