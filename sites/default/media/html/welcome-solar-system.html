A brief tour of the Solar System may be instructive for people who are new to the topic.

In the near future, perhaps we won’t think of ourselves as being from a particular city or country, but from a particular world. We won’t be Australians, Colombians or Kenyans, we’ll be Terrans, Martians and Lunans. And perhaps not long after that, we’ll consider ourselves as citizens of the Solar System, or Alpha Centauri, or some other star system. Some of us will be here for hundreds of years, so we should start getting used to the idea that our home is the Solar System, not just Earth.

Units

Before we start, it’s useful to know that a number of different units are used when talking about distances in the Solar System. These are the most common:

light-second (ls) Distance travelled by light in a vacuum in one second;
299 792 km
gigametre (Gm) 1 000 000 km
light-minute (lmin) Distance travelled by light in a vacuum in one minute;
18 Gm
astronomical unit (AU) Average distance from Earth to the Sun;
150 Gm

Gigametres are a standard SI unit, and therefore useful for certain calculations. Light seconds and light minutes are useful for measuring communication times between different places in the Solar System. Astronomical units are commonly used for measuring the distances between planets and the Sun.

Sometimes “millions of miles” are used for measuring distances in the Solar System, but they really shouldn’t be. Space is international, and miles are not part of any international standard system of units. In addition, miles are only useful for terrestrial scale measurements, whereas, within the International System of Units, prefixes enable the metre to be used for measurements from the nanometre to the gigametre scale and beyond.

Overview

The Solar System has organised itself into three reasonably distinct regions. (The terms used here are all explained in the next chapter.)

  1. The Inner Solar System, which includes:
    • The Sun (also known as Sol) the only star in our star system;
    • The four inner or terrestrial planets:
      • Mercury;
      • Venus;
      • Earth and its moon Luna;
      • Mars and its moons Phobos and Deimos;
    • The Asteroid Belt (between about 2.1 and 3.3 AU), which contains the dwarf planet Ceres and about a million SSSBs (small Solar System bodies);
    • Many other asteroids, including NEAs (Near Earth Asteroids), some of which are also PHAs (Potentially Hazardous Asteroids);
  2. The Outer Solar System, which includes:
    • The four outer or giant planets and associated objects:
      • Jupiter, its 67 moons and 6178 trojans*;
      • Saturn and its 62 moons;
      • Uranus, its 27 moons and 1 trojan;
      • Neptune, its 14 moons and 13 trojans;
    • More than 44 000 centaurs, which are SSSBs with a semi-major axis between Jupiter and Neptune’s orbits (~5.2 < a < ~30.1). At least some of these exhibit characteristics of both asteroids and comets; hence the name.
  3. The Trans-Neptunian region, which includes:
    • The Kuiper Belt, which comprises minor planets in stable orbits between about 30 and 50 AU. It contains:
      • The dwarf planets Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, and others, and their moons.
      • Lots of SSSBs.
    • The Scattered Disc, which comprises minor planets in unstable orbits between about 30 AU and 100 AU. These orbits are unstable because they come close enough to Neptune that its gravity can perturb them. It contains:
      • The dwarf planet Eris, and others, and their moons.
      • Lots of SSSBs, including comets.
    • The Hills Cloud (also known as the Inner Oort Cloud), a hypothetical disc-shaped region that extends between about 100 - 3000 AU and 20 000 to 30 000 AU. It potentially contains billions of minor planets.
    • The Oort Cloud, a hypothetical spherical region that may extend out as far as 100 000 to 200 000 AU. It also may contain billions of minor planets.

* It’s estimated there could be about a million Jupiter trojans larger than 1 km in diameter. Neptune may have 10 - 100 times more large trojans (< 100 km) than Jupiter. No Saturnian trojans have yet been discovered, probably because of Jupiter’s gravity.

Settlement Zones

Each of these three regions can be divided into two “settlement zones”, to make a total of six.

I have assigned each zone a colour:

  • Yellow Zone (approx. 0 to 0.9 AU). This zone encompasses the Sun, Mercury, and Venus. This is the hottest zone and will be the second region of the Solar System that humans begin to settle.
  • Green Zone (approx. 0.9 to 3.7 AU). This zone encompasses Earth, Luna, Mars, and the Asteroid Belt. This is the most habitable and profitable zone, where at least 90% of our activity will be focused during the first major phase of human expansion into space, and probably where at least 70% of humans will always live even once we’ve spread throughout the Solar System. You are here.
  • Red Zone (approx. 3.7 to 14 AU). This zone encompasses the gas giants Jupiter and Saturn. It’s likely to be the more popular region of the Outer Solar System for settlement, and the third zone of the Solar System that we expand into.
  • Blue Zone (approx. 14 to 30.4 AU). This zone encompasses the ice giants Uranus and Neptune. This zone may eventually be settled, but it will take more time and better propulsion, communications, and energy technology. This will be the fourth zone that we settle.
  • Orange Zone (approx. 30.4 to 100 AU). This zone is within the heliopause*, and approximately encompasses the Kuiper Belt and Scattered Disc (these groups don’t have well-defined boundaries). There are only dwarf planets and small Solar System bodies here. Perhaps some will eventually be settled, especially Pluto, but probably not by large numbers of people.
  • Purple Zone (approx. 100+ AU). This zone is beyond the heliopause. This is interplanetary space, approximately encompassing the Hills and Oort Clouds. It is the largest of these zones, the one about which we know the least, and the least likely that humans will ever settle.

* The heliopause is where the pressure of the solar wind is balanced by the stellar winds of other stars. This is where interstellar space is begins, although not necessarily where the Solar System ends.

The Green Zone is the most habitable and valuable in the Solar System. Earth and Mars are the most hospitable planets, Luna will be home to hundreds of thousands of people purely by virtue of its proximity to Earth and the Sun, and the asteroids represent vast material resources for supplying spacecraft and building space infrastructure. We will probably build a base on Ceres, expand asteroid mining throughout the Asteroid Belt, and build colonies inside metallic asteroids. This zone is where the majority of human activity will be, now and into the future.

At some point, probably in the early 22nd century, human activity will spread into the adjacent Yellow and Red Zones.  Our pathway into the Solar System will look like this: Green → Yellow → Red → Blue. Maybe some people (e.g. hardcore plutophiles) will colonise some of the dwarf planets in the Orange Zone in the far future, but probably not the Purple.

Here’s what the distribution of the human population of the Solar System could look like from around 2200:

  • Yellow Zone: 5%
  • Green Zone: 90%
  • Red Zone: 4%
  • Blue Zone: 1%

Assuming we terraform Venus, perhaps by the year 3000 it will look more like this:

  • Yellow Zone: 20%
  • Green Zone: 70%
  • Red Zone: 8%
  • Blue Zone: 2%

Where to settle?

Several key factors will determine our preferences as to where in the Solar System we will settle:

  1. Proximity. To reduce transportation and communication times, we will naturally favour nearby locations. Therefore, we will initially target the Terran System (i.e. the Earth-Luna System), then expand into other parts of the Inner Solar System.
  2. Energy. The Sun is a valuable source of light and heat, which has important psychological benefits in addition to being valuable for photosynthesis and solar power. The Inner Solar System has much more solar energy than the Outer Solar System, which means, until fusion or some other exotic method of energy production is developed, it will probably be favoured.
  3. Resources. Space settlement requires material resources such as water and other volatiles*, metals, carbon, and other elements necessary for life and industry. Thus, planets, moons, and asteroids will be preferred to free space, particularly those rich in the most essential resources such as carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, and oxygen.
  4. Gravity. The optimum gravity level for human exploration will be close to Earth level, or ideally somewhat less, as this will better support the use of spacesuits. If the gravity is too high, then it won’t be practical to work in spacesuits on the surface. If it’s too low, then settlers may suffer excessive bone and muscle loss, potentially preventing them from returning to Earth (at least until the technology is available to solve this problem). With this in mind, terrestrial planets will be preferred, followed by some of the larger dwarf and satellite planets.
  5. Radiation protection. The interplanetary medium is a high radiation environment, which can damage DNA and increase the risk of cancer. We will therefore favour planets and moons with thick atmospheres (Earth, Venus, Titan), living underground (any minor or terrestrial planet, or moon), or farther from the Sun (Callisto, Titan).

* Volatiles are small molecules with low boiling points, normally associated with planetary surfaces or atmospheres, such as hydrogen (H2), helium (He), oxygen (O2), nitrogen (N2), argon (Ar), water (H2O), methane (CH4), carbon dioxide (CO2), carbon monoxide (CO), ammonia (NH3), neon (Ne), sulphur dioxide (SO2), hydrogen sulphide (H2S), hydrogen chloride (HCl), and hydrogen fluoride (HF).

Considering all these factors, the terrestrial planets and asteroids of the Inner Solar System are the most favourable destinations for settlement. Although this is the smallest of the three regions of the Solar System, it’s where everyone currently lives, and even as we expand throughout the Solar System, it will probably always be where most people live.

Mercury and Venus, however, present unique challenges that will defer their settlement. Mercury is baking hot, receiving almost 7 times the solar irradiance of Earth or Luna. Venus is even hotter, with a super-high density atmosphere, and thick clouds that obscure visibility of the surface from space. This is why we focus on Luna and Mars. As we settle Luna and Mars, the asteroid mining will evolve and expand outwards towards the motherlode of the Asteroid Belt, where we will establish bases on Ceres, Psyche, and other asteroids.

The worlds of the Inner Solar System have the advantages of abundant solar energy, proximity to Earth, planets with decent gravity, and abundant resources from asteroids. The Outer Solar System has different advantages, that will become more important in the future: numerous worlds to settle, amazing views of the giant planets, lower radiation (excluding the radiation belts around Jupiter), and abundant resources available from the atmospheres, moons, rings, and trojans of the giant planets.

Communication times

Communication between space settlements and Earth will be a particularly important consideration. I’ve added this little section to expand on this topic, and emphasise why proximity is not only important for reducing time spent in interplanetary space when travelling to and from settlements, but also for communications between settlements and Earth, which will be a much more frequent consideration.

Communication means mission support; information, data, and content; education; participation in human culture, politics, and economics; and connection to friends, family, and humanity as a whole. This has major implications for safety, emergency response, psychological health, and the successful development of the Solar System.

Electromagnetic radiation such as light and radio signals travel at the speed of light, which is about 300 000 km/s or 18 Gm/min. You can therefore calculate the time it takes to send messages between points in space by calculating the light travel time.

For example, the average distance between Earth and Luna is about 384 400 km. Thus, it takes about 1.3 seconds to send a message between Earth and Luna. It can take more than 3 seconds between speaking and hearing a reply, which may be inconvenient, but no worse than international phone calls once were, or VoIP calls between some remote areas are today.

The distance between Earth and Mars varies between approximately 75 to 375 Gm during their 26-month synodic period, which means it takes between about 4 and 21 minutes for a radio signal to travel between Mars and Earth. This makes real-time conversation difficult, as it can take more than 40 minutes between speaking and hearing a reply from Earth.

Real-time conversations are therefore possible between Earth and Luna, as they were during the Apollo missions. However, communications between Earth and Mars will tend towards asynchronous modes of communication such as email. People living on Luna will be able to browse the Earthside web directly, with a delay in page retrieval times comparable to the early days of the internet, although local caches will be employed to reduce this. On Mars, local caches of popular websites and content will be essential; for more obscure content it could mean significant wait times as pages or whole sites are downloaded, and normal web-browsing will not be possible.

Communication speeds may not be limited to light speed for ever. New space communications technologies based on quantum entanglement, for instance, could enable instantaneous communications across interplanetary distances.
