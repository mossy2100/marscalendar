Within a space settlement we must control the atmosphere to certain specifications for optimal functioning of the inhabitants; most importantly, the human ones. What are these specifications? We must consider the settlement atmosphere’s:

  • temperature
  • pressure
  • composition

Terran air has a pressure of 101.325 kPa. The dry atmosphere has a composition as follows:

Gas Percentage (%) Partial pressure (kPa)
Nitrogen (N2) 78.08 79.11
Oxygen (O2) 20.95 21.23
Argon (Ar) 0.93 0.94
Carbon dioxide (CO2) 0.04 0.04
Various other gases Trace Trace
Total 100.00 101.32

Added to this dry atmosphere is a component of water vapour, which varies from 0.001% to 5%, depending on climate and region.

However, we don’t necessarily need a settlement atmosphere that exactly matches Terran air. Humans and other organisms can operate within a range of temperatures and pressures, as long as the atmosphere contains sufficient amounts of the necessary ingredients.

Temperature

In terms of comfort and productivity, what is the optimal temperature for humans?

Scientific standards for Terran atmosphere include:

  • Standard Ambient Temperature and Pressure: 298.15 K (25 °C, 77 °F) and an absolute pressure of 100 kPa.
  • Normal Temperature and Pressure: 293.15 K (20 °C, 68 °F) and absolute pressure of 101.325 kPa.

The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language defines “room temperature” as around 20 to 22 °C (68 to 72 °F).

The West Midlands Public Health Observatory has provided these figures:

  • 24 °C - top range of comfort
  • 21 °C - recommended living room temperature
  • 18 °C - recommended bedroom temperature
  • 16 °C - resistance to respiratory diseases weakened
  • 12 °C - more than two hours at this temperature raises blood pressure and increases heart attack and stroke risk
  • 5 °C - significant risk of hypothermia

Some people like it a little warm, and some like it a little cool. This can depend on where the person comes from, their metabolism, or simply personal preference. We must choose a temperature range such that the maximum number of people will feel comfortable and productive, since we’re designing settlements for people who could come from anywhere on Earth. If it’s too different for them, in terms of temperature or anything else, then at least it should not be too difficult to adapt. In other words, it should not be too cold, nor too hot, but just right.

Given these values and considerations, we probably want to control the internal temperature to a range of about 20 to 25 °C in order for most people to feel comfortable.

Idea

We could map the temperature of the surrounding lunar or Martian environment to the temperature of the settlement atmosphere using a simple linear equation. The maximum temperature outside would correspond to 25 °C inside, and the minimum temperature outside would correspond to 20 °C inside. We would measure the external temperature constantly, and provide that data to the ECLSS computer, which would then calculate a corresponding internal temperature and adjust accordingly using heaters or air conditioners as required.

To illustrate, let’s say that Enterprise is located at a place on Mars where the temperature varies between -90 °C and -10 °C. The internal temperature (Ti) would be calculated from the external temperature (Te) as follows:

Ti = 0.0625 Te + 25.625 °C

The ECLSS would modify the internal temperature to match the calculated value, thus the internal temperature would reflect the external temperature, and everyone would be able to feel it.

The temperature would vary in a noticeable yet not uncomfortable manner during the course of the Martian day and year, in sync with Mars’ diurnal and seasonal cycles (or in the case of Luna, with the monthly cycle). This will give us a direct sensory experience of what the temperature is like outside. We will know when it is a warm day or a cool night at our little spot on Luna or Mars. This will connect us with our adopted world in a very tangible way, and we will begin adapting to its cycles. Also, we will have something to talk about: the weather.

We could use a different equation within an isolated greenhouse, in order to simulate, for example, a temperate environment with seasons of similar temperature as on Earth, but having Martian duration. In this way, temperate zone crops could be trained to adapt to Mars’ longer seasons.

One thing we must consider, however, is that when the internal temperature is high, the atmosphere will expand, which will tend to increase the internal pressure. This may be undesirable depending , We may not want this. However, we can control the internal pressure using pumps that add or remove nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and water vapour as required. We need these anyway, because other processes will also cause gas partial pressures to change, and we need to maintain them at the correct desired levels.

This idea could be applied to greenhouses, with different temperature ranges. Other metrics such as the humidity and pressure outside could potentially also be mapped to the internal environment, which would connect the plants more with Mars. In tropical greenhouses, the climate does not need to vary greatly, but in temperate greenhouses, we may want to simulate an internal climate that tracks the external climate throughout the year, such that the plants and other species will begin to adapt to the longer Martian seasons.

Pressure

A settlement on Luna or Mars must contain pressurised gas at a pressure approximately 100 kPa higher than the external environment. The air inside will therefore be pushing hard against the outer walls, floors and ceilings of the settlement. A space settlement is similar to a submarine in that it must have a strong outer skin, essentially a hull, to contain the pressurised air. The difference is that, with a space settlement, the external pressure is much lower than the internal pressure, instead of much higher. The air inside is pushing strongly outwards against the hull, instead of the water outside pushing strongly inwards. Like a submarine hull, the settlement hull will need to be strong enough to contain the air inside without bursting, or leaking too much.

Historically, two main options have been used when it comes to spacecraft and space station atmospheres:

  1. A lower-pressure atmosphere, comprising either pure oxygen or some combination of oxygen and nitrogen.
  2. An Earthlike atmosphere of about 101 kPa, comprising about 21% oxygen and 79% nitrogen.

Using an Earthlike atmosphere has a couple of specific advantages:

  • No prebreathing required is when transferring from Earth atmosphere to the spacecraft.
  • Equipment designed for an Earthlike atmosphere is more likely to work as expected in the spacecraft. It therefore becomes easier to use COTS (commercial off-the-shelf) hardware, which saves money on development costs. Also, it’s much easier to test equipment on Earth if it’s expected to work in an Earthlike atmosphere in space, because the testing can be done in a workshop, laboratory, someone’s backyard, or wherever; otherwise, a hypobaric chamber is needed.
  • A high percentage of buffer gas greatly reduces the risk of fire.

Despite these advantages, an Earthlike atmosphere probably isn’t optimal for a settlement on Luna or Mars. There are several very good reasons for designing a settlement atmosphere with a lower pressure:

  1. Less material is required for the settlement hull to successfully contain the atmosphere. It will leak less, and be less likely to pop.
  2. Less air needs to be made, saving energy and time.
  3. A lower pressure makes it possible to avoid the need for prebreathing (explained below) before using spacesuits, which we will want to do whenever we want to go outside on Luna or Mars, which presumably many people will want to do, and potentially quite often. Not having to prebreathe saves time, and reduces the risk of decompression sickness. This is called a zero prebreathe protocol.
  4. For Mars, another reason is to more closely match the atmospheric pressure on a terraformed Mars, which is likely to be much lower than Earth’s.

Let’s look at each of these points.

Less material is required

The main reason for building spacecraft and space stations with low-pressure atmospheres is that it means the pressurised modules won’t need to be as strong as if they were designed for a full atmosphere of pressure, which means less material is needed, which means the modules are lighter, which means they’re cheaper to manufacture and to launch into space.

This reasoning is still valid for lunar and Martian settlements. The pressurised structures that we build will have to contain the atmosphere against a very low external pressure. In a sense they will be like a balloon, with air pressing outwards on the walls. This is another big advantage of building underground, as the surrounding rock will be strong enough to contain the internal atmospheric pressure. However, even if the settlement is mostly located underground and encased in rock, there will still be doors, windows, skylights, greenhouses, domes, and so on, that need to be strong enough to contain the internal atmospheric pressure. The lower this pressure, the easier and cheaper it will be to manufacture these items.

Also consider that the lower the atmospheric pressure inside a container, the less likely that it will burst, and the less it will leak. While we will design for zero leaks in the settlement hull, some leakage is inevitable.

Less air to make

For safety, we must be able to make our own air on Luna and Mars. Some air will gradually be lost from the system due to recycling inefficiencies, airlocks, and tiny leaks, and we will have to constantly replace this lost gas. On the ISS, this replacement gas comes from nitrogen and oxygen tanks that are shipped up from Earth. In Serenity and Enterprise, we will also top up the settlement’s atmosphere form nitrogen and oxygen tanks, but we will need to fill these tanks using local resources. That is, after all, the whole point of settling a world rather than somewhere free space. In this way we can be more self-reliant, and our survival will be more assured.

Fortunately, Mars has plenty of oxygen and nitrogen in the atmosphere. Luna also has plenty of oxygen as oxides and in water ice, and nitrogen in the form of ammonia ice. In either case, producing breathable air will cost energy, time, and therefore also money. If the settlement atmosphere is 53 kPa instead of 101 kPa, that’s literally a 47% savings on all these resources whenever we need to inflate an extension to the settlement.

Zero Prebreathe Protocol

There is a second good reason to minimise space settlement atmospheric pressure, which is the opportunity for a zero prebreathe protocol.

To explain. Gas pressurised spacesuits, like the classic Apollo and ISS spacesuits, are essentially rigid balloons, and it’s difficult to move around in them. I asked the astronaut Andy Thomas what it was like, repairing the Hubble, and he said matter-of-factly: “Well, it was very hard work!” Using the gloves is like constantly squeezing a tennis ball. Not only is this difficult and fatiguing, it also compromises dexterity, making it difficult to grip things and easy to drop them. The stiffness of the suit also makes it difficult to bend over, which someone may want to do quite often when working or exploring outside on Luna or Mars.

To minimise this problem, spacesuit atmospheres are designed to have very low pressure, usually comprising pure oxygen.

Transferring from high to low pressure environments can cause bubbles of nitrogen to form in the blood; a condition called decompression sickness, also known as “the bends”. This commonly occurs when underwater divers ascend too quickly. To counteract this effect, they ascend in stages, waiting for a period of time at set depths on their way up, so that their bodies can adjust to the reduced pressure. Preparing for a deep dive means calculating in advance the depths at which the divers should wait on the way back up, and how long to wait for.

The pressure differential between the spacecraft and a spacesuit means that the astronauts can be at risk of decompression sickness when they use the spacesuits. Therefore, before doing so, they will often spend a period of time breathing pure oxygen in order to remove the nitrogen from their blood. This is called prebreathing, and it can take anywhere from 20 minutes to 2 hours.

The amount of prebreathing necessary is determined by a quantity called the R value, which is the ratio between the partial pressure of buffer gas (usually nitrogen) in the spacecraft, and the total pressure in the spacesuit. If this ratio is 1 (i.e. the pressures are the same), then no prebreathing is required; hence the term “zero prebreathe protocol”. If we can achieve this design goal then we’ll be able to go outside as often as we like, without any risk of getting the bends. If the R value is greater than 1, however, then prebreathing will be required. Aside from the wasted time, there would also be a risk of decompression sickness regardless, because this risk is cumulative; it increases with every EVA.

An R value of 1 is therefore an important design goal for Serenity and Enterprise. Let’s consider a person that wants to go outside once a week, for about 50 years (or 27 Martian years) of their life; this is a total of 2500 EVAs! Now consider that Luna and Mars settlers might live for centuries, and some might want or need to go outside every day. We have to expect that some settlers may wish to go outside literally thousands of times during their time on Luna or Mars, and we have to design for that. Since risk of decompression is present with an R value greater than 1, and is cumulative, our design goal must be an R value of 1. Therefore, the spacesuit oxygen pressure must match the buffer gas pressure in the settlement.

Fortunately, there is a new type of spacesuit being developed called a mechanical counter-pressure (MCP) spacesuit. These are skintight, like a diver’s wetsuit. Pressure is applied to the wearer from the tight, elastic material, rather than from gas as in a conventional gas pressurised spacesuit. This makes for a suit which is much easier to move around in.

In an MCP spacesuit, we don’t need to minimise the air pressure as much. One of the latest designs, the Biosuit developed by MIT, will have a pressure around 35 kPa. As we shall see, this is perfect. This value closely matches the amount of nitrogen in the atmosphere design proposed below, which gives us an R value of 1, and thus a zero prebreathe protocol.

Atmospheric pressure on terraformed Mars

When we terraform Mars it’s unlikely that we will attempt to create a full 101 kPa of pressure at sea level on Mars. This is because of the planet’s low gravity, which causes air to weigh less and thus produce less pressure. We need significantly more gas in the atmosphere to produce an atmospheric pressure equal to Earth’s. To create Earth-level atmospheric pressure would require about 2.6 times the mass of atmosphere per square metre compared with Earth, because on Mars a given mass only weighs 38% as much.

If we attempt to build such a massive atmosphere, it would reach actually higher than Phobos.

It will also be much cheaper to build a lower pressure atmosphere. Building Mars’ atmosphere will be an enormous engineering project, involving advanced space technology and the efforts of many scientists and engineers over a period decades. If insufficient nitrogen can be found on Mars with which to build the target atmosphere, then we may have to import it from Venus, Titan, or asteroids. Meeting these requirements will undoubtedly cost billions of sollars (Solar System Dollars).

Creating an atmospheric pressure of 53 kPa (which, as we’ll see, is approximately the minimum safe total pressure) will require 1.4 times the mass of atmosphere per square metre as on Earth. Even this will be extremely difficult.

As the terraforming project produces niches where Terran people, plants, and animals can live, it will be advantageous if the new atmosphere matches the settlement atmosphere that they’ve already adapted to.

Composition

To support the various things living in it, the settlement atmosphere must contain a mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and water vapour. It will contain traces of many other gases as well, but these are the four main ones we care about.

With a design goal of minimising the atmospheric pressure while preserving maximum health and safety, we can calculate the optimal partial pressures of each of these gases.

Oxygen

To minimise the total atmospheric pressure in the settlement, we begin with minimising the oxygen pressure.

The minimum desirable oxygen partial pressure is about 16 kPa. This is approximately what you’ll find in a passenger airplane, or at about 2400 metres altitude. Most people can ascend to this altitude without any problems, although, above this altitude, some people will start showing signs of altitude sickness. It’s perfectly possible to acclimatise to lower pressures, and many people live above this altitude. However, we don’t want people arriving at the settlement from Earth and immediately getting altitude sickness, hence 16 kPa is a good minimum.

Another constraint is that, to mitigate fire risk, we want an oxygen partial pressure no greater than roughly 30% of the total atmospheric pressure. In a high oxygen atmosphere it’s easier for fires to start. This was the cause of the Apollo 1 fire.

The Apollo crew modules originally had high-pressure 100% oxygen atmospheres for when they were on the launch pad. During a test with the Apollo 1 astronauts in the capsule, on the launch pad, a fire started in the cabin. Unfortunately the crew could not escape, and died. The Apollo crew capsule was subsequently redesigned with a different atmosphere for when it was on the launch pad, with 60% oxygen and 40% nitrogen. Nitrogen has the effect of diluting oxygen, and reducing the risk of fire.

If the desired oxygen pressure is 16 kPa (lowest reasonable safe value), and we specify this to be 30% of the total atmospheric pressure, we can calculate the total pressure:

16 kPa / 30% = 53.33 kPa

This is just over half the standard pressure at sea level on Earth.

Some experimentation is necessary to determine the precise effect this will have on gas exchange rates in plants. The low air pressure, and in particular the low partial pressures of carbon dioxide, oxygen, and nitrogen, may affect crop growth rates. However, it’s known that plants can grow in one-tenth of an atmosphere (about 10 kPa), and some space greenhouses are designed for low pressures like this.

It’s fortunate that we are able to design a safe atmosphere for the settlements that has a much lower pressure than Earth’s, because less gas, time, money and energy will be required to inflate new sections of the settlement build the atmosphere, plus the various health and safety benefits.

Carbon dioxide

Humans don’t need carbon dioxide to breathe, but we’ll be sharing our quarters with plants, which do. Even if we create separate, sealed environments for greenhouses and farm modules, we will still probably want some indoor plants about the place to make it look nice.

Fortunately, they don’t need much. While plants grow faster when there’s more CO2 in the air, humans start to get drowsy when concentrations reach about 1%. Symptoms worsen with increased CO2 concentration.

It’s typical to maintain a maximum CO2 concentration in spacecraft atmospheres of about 5000 ppm (0.5%). However, while this may be tolerable for trained astronauts, this will be too high for civilians.

We need to keep CO2 concentrations low enough that it doesn’t bother us, but high enough that plants can absorb it from the air. Terran atmosphere currently contains about 400 ppm CO2, however, for all of human history prior to the Industrial Revolution it ranged between about 180 and 280 ppm, so we know these levels are plenty for healthy plants.

Indoor concentrations tend to be higher, depending on the level of ventilation. Organisations such as ASHRAE (American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers) and OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration) specify a maximum level of 1000 ppm for areas like classrooms and conference rooms, and a maximum of 5000 ppm for up to 8 hours.

However, anything above about 600 ppm can become uncomfortable, and many people will be living in this atmosphere 24.66 hours per sol, 7 sols per Martian week.

More CO2 means healthier plants, but we wish for everyone to feel comfortable indoors. Therefore, we should probably aim to keep CO2 levels around 500 ppm. Our target atmosphere will therefore include about:

53.33 kPa * 500 ppm = 0.03 kPa of CO2

Water vapour

Terran air contains a little water vapour, which our respiration and perspiration systems are adapted to. We require some water vapour in the air because overly dry air can cause dry skin and respiratory issues.

Plants like it, too. Water vapour condenses on their leaves, and sinks into the soil where it can be taken up by the roots.

The amount of water vapour in the atmosphere is expressed as humidity, a percentage value that ranges from 0% (totally dry) to 100% (saturated). In a desert it may be 0% humidity or close to it. In a steamy, tropical rainforest or a jungle, or when it’s raining, the humidity is 100%. Low humidity makes you feel cooler, and high humidity makes you feel hotter.

NASA specifies a humidity in the range of 30 - 70% inside spacecraft, which is a comfortable range. The optimal range is around 45 - 55%. Saturation pressure varies with temperature. At 22.5 °C, which will be the average temperature inside the settlement, the saturated vapour pressure will be 2.73 kPa. For a target humidity of 50%, our atmosphere will therefore include about:

2.73 kPa * 50% = 1.37 kPa of H2O

Nitrogen and argon

Nitrogen and argon have the effect of diluting atmospheric oxygen, which makes combustion reactions more difficult, thus reducing the possibility of fire hazards. For this reason they are referred to as buffer gases. In spacecraft atmospheres, only nitrogen is used for buffer gas. In Earth’s atmosphere the buffer gases comprise about 99% nitrogen and 1% argon.

Nitrogen will probably be the only buffer gas in lunar settlements, because it will be much easier to source than argon. There is a tiny bit of argon in the lunar atmosphere and a tiny bit in the regolith, but the polar cold traps, which we will mine for water, contain nitrogen in the form of ammonia ice.

In Martian settlements we may wish to include argon in our buffer gas, as it's present in the atmosphere in approximately the same proportion as nitrogen. Mars’ atmosphere contains 1.89% nitrogen and 1.93% argon.

In my previous book I explained how a Mars base’s atmosphere could use a mixture of nitrogen and argon as buffer gas, as this would be energetically cheaper to produce. As both gases have rather low boiling points, separating these gases from each other using fractional distillation would require a lot of energy, so it’s cheaper to extract these gases together from the atmosphere rather than separating them. Since we can safely breathe argon, and because it’s also a buffer gas, this would be a more cost-effective approach than extracting pure nitrogen.

However, at Enterprise we will want to produce pure nitrogen and argon for industrial purposes. Nitrogen will be required for manufacturing chemicals such as ammonia, and argon may be required for welding, lighting, or lasers. Therefore, we will utilise a more advanced air separation system that uses cryogenic distillation to produce pure carbon dioxide, nitrogen, argon, and perhaps other gases, from the Martian atmosphere. This will enable us to use pure nitrogen as the settlement's buffer gas, which will be more compatible with the physiology of humans and other Terran species in the settlement. Argon could still be utilised as a buffer gas in the event of a nitrogen shortage.

The nitrogen partial pressure (ppN2) will be whatever is left over to make up the 53.33 kPa total, so we can calculate it:

ppN2 = pTotal - ppO2 - ppCO2 - ppH2O

 = (53.33 - 16.00 - 0.03 - 1.37) kPa

 = 35.93 kPa

This completes the atmosphere design.

For an R value of 1, the spacesuit pressure must match the nitrogen gas pressure in the settlement, which in this design is about 36 kPa. Fortunately, the mechanical counter-pressure spacesuits should be able to match this pressure, which is a very satisfactory result.

There is an important reason why we should make the atmosphere in Serenity the same or very similar as in Enterprise. It will then be much easier for people to move from one settlement to the other, which many people, especially the actual construction enginauts, may want or need to do. The different gravity will still be a challenge, but at least if the atmospheres in each settlement have the same total and oxygen pressures, it will be that much easier to adapt.

Farm and greenhouse atmospheres

Farm and greenhouse atmospheres could potentially be optimised for plant growth rates. For example, while humans will begin to feel sleepy in an atmosphere with 1% CO2, plants will grow more quickly at higher concentrations of CO2. Therefore, our farm and greenhouse areas will benefit from having their own environment control system, separate from the human-populated areas of the settlement.

Of course, people still need to enter these areas and work. Recall that current US standards specify a maximum concentration of 5000 ppm for up to 8 hours. Although this will be 10 times the concentration in the primary settlement atmosphere, it would be a safe level for people working in the greenhouses, even for a few hours, and the extra CO2 will improve crop production.

Temperatures in farm and greenhouse areas may also vary from the settlement, designed to suit the types of crops inside. For example, we may create tropical greenhouses and temperate greenhouses.

In order to limit the amount of CO2 leakage from the greenhouses into the main settlement, hard seals will be needed between human areas and agricultural areas. A double door system, similar to an airlock, should be sufficient for this purpose.
