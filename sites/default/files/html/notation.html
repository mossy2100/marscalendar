Dates and datetimes

The standard notations for Utopian dates and datetimes are similar to the international standard ISO 8601 for Gregorian equivalents, with a few differences:

  • A date or datetime string begins with ‘M’, to indicate mir or Mars date.
  • Mir numbers do not need to be zero-padded to a fixed number of digits. The current mir number (216 at the time of writing) has 3 digits, but this is written as M216, not M0216. When it reaches 1000, it will be M1000. This is for future-proofing. Eventually mir numbers will have 4 digits, 5 digits, and so on. It’s actually a flaw in ISO 8601 that years must be expressed as exactly four digits, because the standard will not work from the year 10 000. The authors of the standard created a Y10K problem. In my opinion they should remove this restriction from the standard.
  • Month and sol parts of the date also don’t need to be zero-padded to 2 digits. e.g. M216/1/5
  • Negative mir numbers are permitted, e.g. M-100/10/5
  • A date and time can be combined into a single datetime string, e.g. M216/17/22 .12345. Times within a datetime string are displayed as a decimal fraction of a sol, usually to 3, 5, or 6 decimal places, to indicate mils, tals, or microsols respectively, depending on the desired resolution, e.g. M216/1/5.12345
  • The date separator character is ‘/’. On Earth we use hyphen (or minus sign) ‘-’, forward slash ‘/’, and period (or decimal point) ‘.’ characters as date separators, depending on the country. The ISO 8601 standard uses the hyphen (e.g. “2016-12-06”). However, the hyphen and period characters have other meanings within the format, as a negative sign and a decimal point respectively, therefore we should choose an alternative for clarity. The forward slash is the most obvious choice, as it clearly indicates a date when used in this way. A different character could potentially be used, e.g. 216^17^22 or 216*17*22, but this might be tedious to write, less likely to be adopted, and unclear as to its meaning (e.g. potentially interpreted as a mathematical operator). The ‘M’ prefix will prevent confusion with Terran dates better than an unusual separator.
  • Thousands and thousandths separators are not used and are invalid.

Thus, the complete format description for a Utopian date or datetime looks like:

M{date}[{time}]

Where:

{date} = {mir}/{month}/{sol}

{mir} = an integer (negative, 0, or positive)

{month} = an integer in the range 1 to 24

{sol} = an integer in the range 1 to 28

{time} = .{digits} (i.e. a decimal point followed by at least one digit)

The {time} part is optional.

Examples:

Date: M216/17/8

Date in negative mir: M-32/17/8

Datetime, resolution mils: M216/17/8.123

Datetime, resolution tals: M216/17/8.12345

Datetime, resolution microsols: M216/17/8.123456

Presumably the ISO 8601 standard requires date and time parts to have fixed widths in order to simplify parsing, but the above format is easily parsed and more compact.

Long date format

The long date format looks like:

M{mir} {monthName} {sol}

For example:

M216 Tucana 24

The ‘M’ prefix in front of the mir indicates the mir, and that it is a Martian date, and eliminates confusion about which number refers to the mir and which refers to the sol.

Time format

A time by itself is expressed as mils, zero-padded to 3 digits, and, optionally, a decimal point followed by tals or microsols. It is prefixed with a small ‘m’ to indicate mils (or Mars time).

m{mils}[.{digits}]

Examples:

Time, resolution mils: m012

Time, resolution tals: m012.34

Time, resolution microsols: m012.345

Comparing Earth times of day with Mars:

00:00 = m000

06:00 = m250

12:00 = m500

18:00 = m750
