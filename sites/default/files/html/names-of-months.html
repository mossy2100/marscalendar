The names of the months in the Utopian Calendar is one of the only ways that it differs from the Darian Calendar.

When I was designing the calendar, I liked Tom’s idea of using constellations, and of using western and eastern names in order to provide cultural balance. The names he uses are pretty cool, however, I felt that this approach didn’t really solve the issue of cultural bias effectively. Chinese astronomical tradition, for instance, isn’t represented in this scheme.

I decided to depart from the zodiacal constellations and embrace any of the 88 modern constellations as potential names. I felt that these names were fairly well known worldwide (at least by astronomers), and were suitable for a modern scientific calendar. Therefore, instead of the 12 Sanskrit constellations, I selected 12 other names from the current 88 constellations. First, I added Ophiuchus, the 13th zodiacal constellation. Then I discovered that there are 11 constellations with stars brighter than the brightest star in any of the zodiacal constellations, which fit nicely.

However, these are not the names presented here. The only reason I mention this scheme is because those names were the ones I published back in 2000 in the Kepler Calendar, and from there they propagated and became utilised in other places, such as the Martian Calendar app by Laurie Harrison.

When I reviewed the calendar recently, I wasn’t happy with these names, thinking that these were really some of the more boring constellation names. Also, it seemed to be a problem to have months with two-word names like “Canis Major” and “Canis Minor”. I then reviewed the list of 88 constellation names and selected 24 constellations that represent animals or mythical creatures, avoiding two-word names, and constellations that represented people or things

The result is that each month has its own mascot, a symbolic animal or creature, and people will enjoy identifying with the animal or creature associated with the month they are born in. Animal symbolism has played a part in many Earth cultures throughout time, and continues to do so today, in logos, sports teams, and many other contexts. It’s my opinion that, while this is perhaps a less scientific approach, it’s much more creative, fun, and colourful. Surely this is at least part of the reason why so many people have an interest in their astrological and Chinese signs. I also believe that this naming system will help to introduce people to many new constellations, other than the well-known 12 of the zodiac.

The mathematics of the calendar is the science part; the names are the art. As I said to Tom back when we decided to align the calendars, it’s the maths that matters; the names of months and sols of the week are just the paint job. The Martians may come up with different names for their months, but I hope they will like these.

The names have been ordered by right ascension of the constellations.
