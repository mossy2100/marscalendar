Tropical and calendar mirs

The average length of a year in the Gregorian Calendar (the most commonly used calendar on Earth) is 365.2425 days. This is very close to the average period between northern hemisphere vernal equinoxes, which is 365.242 374 days. The same approach is used in the Darian and Utopian Calendars.

The vernal equinox is precise moment that defines the beginning of spring in the northern hemisphere. At this moment, Earth’s axis is side-on to the Sun, and the day will have an equal period of darkness and light. After the northern vernal equinox, nights will begin getting shorter in the northern hemisphere, and longer in the southern.

This particular seasonal marker is chosen because it’s when astronomers mark the beginning of the astronomical year, when Ls = 0°. “Ls” refers to the longitude of the Sun, which means the position of the Sun against the backdrop of fixed stars from the perspective of a location on Earth, Mars, or elsewhere in the Solar System. During the year, Ls increases to 360°.

The average period between northern vernal equinoxes on Mars is 668.5907 sols, and this is used as the basis for the length of the calendar mir length in the Darian and Utopian Calendars. The calendar mir is a whole number of sols, just like the calendar year on Earth is a whole number of days.

Short and long mirs

To ensure the average calendar mir length is 668.5907 sols, a pattern of “short” mirs of 668 sols and “long” mirs of 669 sols is defined. The adjectives “common” and “leap”, which we use with years, aren’t suitable for mirs because short mirs occur less frequently than long mirs.

The Darian calendar specifies a system of rules similar to those used by the Gregorian Calendar to determine when a calendar mir is short or long. These rules produce an average calendar mir length of 668.591 sols per mir, which is very close to the average northern vernal equinox mir:

  1. A mir is short unless one of the following rules applies. (668 sols/mir)
  2. Odd-numbered mirs are long. (668 + 0.5 = 668.5 sols/mir)
  3. Mirs divisible by 10 (ending in 0) are also long. (668.5 + 0.1 = 668.6 sols/mir)
  4. Except, mirs divisible by 100 (ending in 00) are short. (668.6 - 0.01 = 668.59 sols/mir)
  5. Except, mirs divisible by 1000 (ending in 000) are long. (668.59 + 0.001 = 668.591 sols/mir)

There is a difference of 668.591 - 668.5907 = 0.0003 sols, or about 27 seconds, per mir. After 2000 mirs, the error will have accumulated to about 0.6 sols (almost 15 hours), after which time the intercalation rules change.

In 2006 Tom extended the intercalation rules for the first 10 000 mirs, to allow for the slowly increasing length of the Martian northern vernal equinox mir:

Range of mirs Formula Mean length of calendar mir (sols)
0 to 2000 (M − 1)\2 + M\10 − M\100 + M\1000 668.5910
2001 to 4800 (M − 1)\2 + M\10 − M\150 668.5933
4801 to 6800 (M − 1)\2 + M\10 − M\200 668.5950
6801 to 8400 (M − 1)\2 + M\10 − M\300 668.5967
8401 to 10000 (M − 1)\2 + M\10 − M\600 668.5983

Only the 4th and 5th rules (above) need to be altered, and only every 2000 mirs, with the result that the error is only about 1 sol after 12 000 mirs.

The extra or intercalary sol, the 669th sol of the mir in a long mir, is added to the last month of the mir. Aside from the logic and benefits of doing this, it reflects the early Roman calendar, when March was the first month of the year. (January and February were added to the original 10-month calendar around 713 BC. As the standard calendar year was only 355 days, it would fall out of sync with the seasons, so the Romans would periodically add extra days into February to resynchronise it. February was the last month of the year up until about 450 BC, when it became the second month.)

Start of the mir

As above, the calendar mir begins at (within a sol of) the northern hemisphere vernal equinox, when Ls = 0°. This marks the beginning of the spring in the northern hemisphere.

There is a poetic bonus gained by choosing to begin the mir with the northern spring. On Earth the northern spring begins in March, with the vernal equinox occurring on or close to March 20. In the early Roman calendar March was the first month of the year; thus, the year ended with winter and began with spring. March was named for the god Mars, who was originally the Roman god of springtime before he became the god of war. Thus, it’s quite appropriate to begin the new mir at the beginning of spring, which is the god Mars’ season.
